# Monte-Carlo, with units attached

`@gcu/numbat` carries uncertainty the same way it carries dimensions. Build a
distribution, do ordinary arithmetic, and the spread propagates through —
**keeping its unit the whole way**. Collapse to a number when you want one.

/// adder
from numbat import Q, normal, mean, stdev, percentile

# an uncertain ore grade and rock density
grade   = normal(Q(1.2, "g/t"),    Q(0.15, "g/t"))
density = normal(Q(2.7, "g/cm^3"), Q(0.1,  "g/cm^3"))
grade

/// md
An uncertain quantity renders as `mean ± stdev unit`. Now flow it through a real
calculation — tonnage of a block, then contained metal:

/// adder
volume = Q(50000, "m^3")
tonnes = (volume * density).to("t")
metal  = (tonnes * grade).to("kg")
metal

/// md
## Collapse to the numbers you report

The mean, the spread, and a conservative lower percentile (P10) — all still in
kilograms of metal:

/// adder
mean(metal)

/// adder
percentile(metal, 10)

/// md
Because the uncertainty rode through `volume × density × grade`, the P10 already
reflects the combined effect of both uncertain inputs — no separate error
budget, no unit bookkeeping.

/// md
## Other distributions

`uniform(lo, hi)`, `lognormal(mu, sigma)`, and `triangular(lo, mode, hi)` are all
available and compose the same way.

/// adder
from numbat import uniform
recovery  = uniform(Q(0.88), Q(0.94))    # mill recovery, 88–94%, as a fraction
recovered = (metal * recovery).to("kg")
percentile(recovered, 10)
